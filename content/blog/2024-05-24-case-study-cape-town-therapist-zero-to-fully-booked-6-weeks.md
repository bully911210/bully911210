Priya Naidoo is a registered therapist in Cape Town. She qualified in 2020, opened a practice, and spent the next three years building her client list primarily through referrals from the therapist she had trained under and from word-of-mouth in her community.

By the end of 2023, she was seeing about eight clients per week. Her schedule had room for twelve. The gap between eight and twelve represented approximately R8,400 in monthly fees that she was not earning, not because she was not good at her work, but because the four potential clients who could have filled those slots were finding other therapists online and she was not there to be found.

---

## Before: the invisible practice

Priya had three points of online presence before she came to me. She had a Psychology Today profile, a basic Google Business listing with no website linked, and she was listed on a therapy-matching app that she paid R400 per month for and which had sent her two clients in eighteen months.

She had thought about a website many times. Her hesitation was not price. It was uncertainty about what a website would actually do for a therapy practice. Her thinking was that clients needed to trust their therapist personally, and that a website might feel impersonal or clinical.

I want to address this directly, because it is a concern I hear from service providers in personal-trust industries (therapists, coaches, physiotherapists, financial advisers) regularly. It is based on a misunderstanding of how people actually search for therapists.

When someone searches for a therapist in their suburb on Google, they are not looking for a faceless corporate listing. They are looking for a person. A well-built therapist website that shows a real photo, describes the therapist's approach in their own voice, and explains what a first session involves does not feel impersonal. It feels like meeting someone before you meet them. It reduces the anxiety of making the first call.

The psychology-Today profile Priya had was a form she had filled in. It listed her qualifications and her modalities. It was accurate and impersonal. A website in her voice is different.

---

## The build: what we did and why

The site had four pages: a homepage, a services page (which listed each therapy type she offered separately rather than as a combined list), an about page with a full professional biography written in Priya's voice, and a booking page with a form that routed to WhatsApp and prompted visitors to suggest two or three times that worked for them.

Three decisions in the build structure mattered:

**Service-specific pages rather than a combined services list.** When someone searches "trauma-focused CBT Cape Town" or "anxiety therapist Claremont", a page dedicated to that service ranks for that search. A combined services page ranks weakly for all services and strongly for none of them. Priya's services page was actually five pages: one for each therapy modality she practised, each with its own URL structured as /therapy-type-cape-town and each with LocalBusiness schema identifying her as a therapist in Cape Town.

**The about page was written as a personal narrative, not a CV.** The about page is the most visited page on a therapist's website, typically more visited than the services page. People read it to decide whether they can trust this person with difficult things. Priya's about page talked about why she became a therapist, what her sessions feel like, and what she believes about the therapeutic process. This is not content I wrote for her. I interviewed her for 40 minutes and wrote from those notes in her voice.

**The booking form went directly to WhatsApp.** A person who fills in a therapy enquiry form at 9pm on a Tuesday is at a particular moment of decision. They may not be there at 9am on Wednesday. The form message arriving immediately on Priya's phone gave her the option to respond that night with a warm, human message that confirmed she had received the enquiry and would be in touch. Most therapist websites I have seen route enquiries to an inbox that gets checked intermittently. The response time matters enormously in this category.

---

## After: six weeks to fully booked

In the first six weeks after launch, Priya's Search Console showed 284 impressions for therapy-related searches in her Cape Town suburb. She received 19 enquiries through the website in that period. Fourteen became consultations. Three became ongoing retained clients generating recurring monthly revenue.

By week six, her twelve available slots were full. By week eight, she had begun a waiting list.

One of those three retained clients brought R8,400 in fees over the first three months. The website cost R1,999 once-off and R299 per month. The first retained client covered the total 24-month cost of the website in three months.

Priya had waited three years before getting a website. During those three years, she was seeing eight clients per week when her capacity was twelve. The four-client gap, at her rate, represented approximately R4,480 per month in revenue she was not earning.

Over 36 months, that is R161,280 in foregone revenue. The maths of that decision is left as an exercise for the reader.

## The bridge: what specifically changed

The difference between Priya's situation before and after the website was not that she was suddenly better at her job. She had always been good at her job.

What changed was that Google could now answer the question "I need a therapist in Cape Town who specialises in anxiety" with Priya's name instead of someone else's.

Before the site: Google had no structured information about Priya as a therapist in Cape Town. She was not in Google's model of the therapy landscape in her suburb.

After the site: Google had LocalBusiness schema asserting she was a registered therapist in Cape Town. It had five service-specific pages with suburb-level keywords. It had a mobile PageSpeed score of 93. It had a Google Business Profile linked to the website. It had real content in a real person's voice that gave Google enough context to understand what kind of practice this was.

That change took four days to build.
