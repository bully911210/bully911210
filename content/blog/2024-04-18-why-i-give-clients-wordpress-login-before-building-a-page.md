You have been burned by a developer before. You paid a deposit. They built something. They asked for more money before they would hand over access. Or they built something and you could not change a word of it without calling them. Or they disappeared after the build and when something broke there was no one to contact.

I hear this story, in some variation, from roughly four out of every ten people who enquire about a website. Sometimes the amount lost was R2,000. Sometimes it was R18,000. The common thread is the same: the client never fully owned what they paid for.

I changed one thing early on that seems, based on the reactions I get, to be unusual in this industry. I give clients their WordPress login before I have built a single page.

Not after the site is finished. Not after the invoice is paid. Before the intake call. Before I have asked you what colour scheme you prefer or what services to list first. The first thing you receive from me after booking is an email with your WordPress admin login.

## Why this matters more than it sounds

WordPress is open source software. It is not owned by any company. It is maintained by a global community of developers and it powers approximately 43 percent of all websites on the internet. Any developer in the world can work on a WordPress site. Any hosting provider in the world can host one. The software is free and its architecture is documented publicly.

When I set up your WordPress installation and give you the login, what I am giving you is not a credential to a service I control. I am giving you administrative access to software that you own, installed on a server that hosts your domain. From the moment you receive that login, the site is yours. I am a contractor working on your property.

This is a different relationship than the one most web developers offer. Most developers, consciously or not, position themselves as the owner of the process. The client is a stakeholder who will receive the finished product at the end. The developer controls access until certain conditions are met.

I do not understand the logic of the other way. If I am building something for you, on infrastructure registered in your name, using open-source software that belongs to nobody, why would I withhold access to it?

## What happens if I disappear

This is the question I get asked more than almost any other, phrased in various ways. What happens if you get sick? What happens if your business closes? What happens if you move overseas?

The answer is the same regardless of what happens to me.

Your WordPress site is backed up automatically every 24 hours to your own Google Drive account. Not my Google Drive. Yours. The backup is a complete copy of the site: the database, the theme files, the plugin configurations, the uploaded images, all of it. Restore from a backup takes about fifteen minutes for a competent WordPress developer.

Any developer in the world can take the backup and restore it to any hosting provider in the world. The plugins I use (Rank Math, WP Rocket, Google Site Kit) are all widely used, well-documented tools with large user bases. Finding a developer who can work on your specific configuration is not difficult.

The admin email is yours. The domain registrar login is yours. The hosting account is yours. The Google Search Console is connected to your Google account, not mine.

If I disappear tomorrow, nothing about your website changes. You might need to find someone to handle ongoing maintenance, which is the normal state of affairs for any piece of infrastructure you own.

## Why the deposit model is backwards

Most developers ask for a deposit of 50 percent or more before starting. This is understandable from a cash flow perspective. It is less understandable from a trust-building perspective.

The deposit model says: "Trust me with your money before you have seen any of my work."

The login-first model says: "Here is your property. I will now build something on it. You have full visibility and control throughout the process."

I have found that clients who receive their login before the intake call approach the project differently. They are not waiting to receive something. They are watching something being built on infrastructure they already own. The psychology of this is different from the start.

Thabo, an IT systems consultant in Johannesburg who reviewed his site after completion, looked at the technical setup and told me unprompted that the configuration was correct. He named Rank Math, WP Rocket, and Google Site Kit specifically and said the PageSpeed score of 92 was higher than he had expected. Thabo builds his own systems for a living. He checked what I had done and confirmed it was done properly. I did not tell him his site was technically excellent. He arrived at that conclusion himself because he had access to examine it.

## The backup you never think about until you need it

I want to say something specific about the Google Drive backup because it is one of those things that seems trivial until it is not.

Load shedding causes server issues occasionally. Hosting providers have infrastructure problems. Plugins can conflict and cause a site to stop loading. When this happens on a site that is backed up to your Google Drive, the conversation with a developer is: "Here is the backup from yesterday, please restore it." That conversation takes about an hour to resolve.

When it happens on a site with no backup, the conversation is much longer and more expensive.

The backup is not a feature I offer because it is a nice-to-have. It is a feature I require because losing a client's site due to a recoverable problem is not something I am willing to let happen.
