Your website has been live for 14 months. You have received two enquiries through it. Both were people who already knew you and could have sent a WhatsApp. You are not entirely sure the website is doing anything.

I want to explain to you the exact difference between what you have and what a website that actually works looks like, because the gap is specific and fixable and not as expensive as you probably think.

---

## The passive website

A passive website exists. It has pages. It has your services listed. It has a contact form that may or may not be delivering enquiries. It was built at some point in the past and has not changed since.

The passive website scores between 35 and 55 on Google PageSpeed Mobile. It has no schema markup. Its meta descriptions are either auto-generated or blank. Its contact form routes through a shared mail relay with a 40 to 60 percent spam delivery rate. Its Google Analytics has either never been set up or shows data that nobody looks at.

The passive website is not failing because the content is bad. It is failing because the technical foundation tells Google nothing specific about the business, and Google responds by ranking it poorly for the searches that matter.

---

## The working website

A working website has four characteristics that a passive website does not.

**It is technically fast.** A mobile PageSpeed score above 85 is not a nice-to-have. It is a direct ranking input. Google's Core Web Vitals algorithm measures how quickly your largest piece of content renders on a phone screen, how stable the page layout is as it loads, and how quickly the page responds to the first tap. Sites that pass these tests rank above sites that do not, all other things being equal. Sites that score 88 to 95 on PageSpeed mobile pass these tests. Sites that score 41 do not.

**It speaks to Google in the right language.** LocalBusiness schema is a piece of structured data added to the homepage and key pages that tells Google the following: the name of this business, the type of business it is, the physical location it serves, the operating hours, the phone number, the service area. Without this schema, your site is a page about plumbing, or therapy, or accounting. With it, your site is Jaco Venter, plumber, Centurion, open Monday to Saturday from 7am to 7pm. Google searches for exactly this kind of certainty when deciding which local business to surface for a location-specific search.

**It delivers its leads in real time.** When someone fills in the contact form on a working website at 10pm on a Sunday, the business owner gets a WhatsApp message on their phone within 60 seconds. No mail server. No spam filter. No Monday morning inbox check. The lead arrives when it is still warm, when the potential client is still at their phone, when responding immediately produces a conversion rate that waiting 12 hours does not.

**It shows you the data.** A working website is connected to Google's own measurement tools. Every week you can see how many people searched for your service in your area, how many saw your site in the results, how many clicked, what they searched for, and where on the page they ended up. This data changes how you make decisions. It shows you which services are being searched for most often in your area. It tells you when a page is ranking at position 14 and therefore needs improvement. It is the difference between guessing and knowing.

## What a working website costs in the real world

Her website cost R1,999 and went live in four days. In the first six weeks, her Google Search Console showed 284 impressions for therapy-related searches in her Cape Town suburb. She received 19 enquiries through the site. Fourteen of those became consultations. Three of those became ongoing retained clients.

By week six, she was fully booked. By week eight, she had a waiting list.

I did not tell Priya what results to expect. I told her what the site would do technically and what that meant for how Google would see her. The bookings came from people she had never met, who had never heard of her, who searched for a therapist in Cape Town and found her because her site was the one that answered Google's questions correctly.

## The revenue transfer frame

Here is the thing about passive websites that I think is worth saying plainly.

Every month your website sits passively while Google ranks it nowhere, someone in your area is searching for exactly what you offer. That person is finding someone else. Not because your competitor is better at your trade. Because their website speaks to Google correctly and yours does not.

This is not theoretical. It is arithmetic. Searches are not lost. They resolve somewhere. Every local search that your passive website misses is a potential client who called someone else.

The working website does not create demand. It captures demand that already exists and is currently going somewhere else.
