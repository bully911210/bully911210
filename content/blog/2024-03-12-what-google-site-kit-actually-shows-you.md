You have a website. You are not sure if anyone is visiting it. You check your email occasionally to see if an enquiry has come in. It has not. You assume the site is not working, but you have no actual data to confirm or contradict that assumption.

This is the default state of most South African small business websites. Not because the owners are uninterested. Because nobody ever showed them where the data is.

Google Site Kit changes this, and I install it on every single WordPress site I build. Here is exactly what it shows you and why it matters.

---

## What Google Site Kit actually is

Google Site Kit is a free WordPress plugin made by Google. It connects your WordPress dashboard directly to three of Google's own measurement tools: Google Search Console, Google Analytics, and Google PageSpeed Insights. Once connected, you do not need to log into three separate Google products to understand your site's performance. Everything appears inside the WordPress admin panel you already use.

This sounds like a small convenience. It is not. For a South African small business owner who did not go to university to study digital marketing, having all this data in one place, in plain language, is the difference between understanding your site and ignoring it.

---

## What Search Console shows you (and why it is the most important thing)

Search Console is the data source that most small business owners have never seen. It shows you precisely what Google knows about your site from Google's perspective, not from a third-party tool, not from an estimate. From Google's own index.

The four numbers that matter most:

**Impressions** tell you how many times your website appeared in Google search results in a given period. If your site had 500 impressions last month, 500 people saw your site listed in their search results. They may not have clicked. But they saw it.

**Clicks** tell you how many of those impressions resulted in someone actually visiting your site. If you had 500 impressions and 25 clicks, your click-through rate is 5 percent. For a local service business, 3 to 8 percent is a normal range. Below 2 percent typically means your meta description and page title are not giving people enough reason to click.

**Average position** tells you where your site appears on average in search results. Position 1 to 3 gets between 30 and 60 percent of clicks. Position 11 onwards gets less than 1 percent. If you are averaging position 15 for your most important search terms, you know exactly what needs to improve.

**Queries** tell you the exact words people typed into Google before your site appeared. This is the data that most small business owners find astonishing the first time they see it. You discover that people are searching for "plumber Centurion emergency" more often than "plumber Centurion", which tells you that an emergency service page might be worth creating. You discover that nobody is searching for the formal name you gave your service, but 40 people per month are searching for a specific type of that service using a different phrase. This data is available to you for free. Most business owners have simply never had it surfaced.

## What Google Analytics shows you

Where Search Console tells you about visibility in Google, Google Analytics tells you about behaviour on your site.

The numbers that matter for a small business:

**Sessions** tell you how many visits your site received in a period. Each session is one visit, regardless of how many pages that visitor looked at.

**Bounce rate** tells you the percentage of visitors who arrived on your site and left without interacting. A very high bounce rate on a services page often means the page took too long to load, the content did not immediately match what the visitor expected, or the call to action was buried.

**Time on page** tells you whether people are actually reading. A visitor who spends 45 seconds on your services page is engaged. A visitor who spends 4 seconds left before reading anything.

**Traffic source** tells you whether visitors are arriving from Google search (organic), from social media, from direct URL typing, or from other websites linking to yours. For most local service businesses, organic Google search should be the primary channel. If most of your traffic is direct or social, it means your site is reaching people who already know you, but is not yet being found by people searching for your category.

## The PageSpeed integration

Site Kit also pulls your PageSpeed Insights score directly into your dashboard. Every week you can see whether your scores on mobile and desktop have changed, and if they have dropped, you know to investigate before the drop affects your rankings.

For context, the SA Business Online Starter sites consistently score between 88 and 95 on mobile at launch. I have not built one that dropped below 82 in the first six months, because WP Rocket's caching configuration and the image optimisation process maintain the score over time.

## The moment that matters

I have watched business owners see their Site Kit dashboard for the first time. Not every client. But enough to know the pattern.

They have had a website for months or years. They have never seen this data. They click into Search Console and see that last month their site had 87 impressions. They had no idea. They see that someone searched for "accountant Randburg tax returns" and their site appeared at position 14. They see that they are receiving clicks on a page they thought nobody visited.

This moment changes something. Not because the numbers are always good. Because the numbers are real. For the first time, the website is not an abstract thing that either works or does not work. It is a measurable thing with specific levers they can pull.

If your current website is connected to Google Site Kit and you can see this data, you are already ahead of most of your competitors. If your website has no data connected to it and you are operating blind, you are optimising by guesswork.
