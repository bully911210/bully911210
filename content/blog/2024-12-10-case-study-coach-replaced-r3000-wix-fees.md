Sipho Ndlovu is a leadership and executive coach based in Johannesburg. He has a strong corporate client base, a well-regarded methodology, and a track record of repeat business from companies that have seen results.

He also had a Wix Business plan that was costing him R3,000 per month.

---

## Before: paying for a platform to own your own content

Sipho had been on Wix Business for two and a half years. He had originally chosen the Business plan because he wanted to offer an online payment option for individual coaching packages and Wix's e-commerce functionality was available on that tier.

He was using the payment functionality irregularly. Most of his corporate clients paid via invoice in any case. But the plan kept renewing, and Sipho had not re-evaluated it.

At R3,000 per month, his Wix investment over 30 months was R90,000. He had a website that scored 39 on Google PageSpeed Mobile, had no LocalBusiness schema, and had a contact form that he had never confirmed was actually delivering enquiries. His organic search presence for coaching-related searches in Johannesburg was effectively zero.

When he came to me, he was not dissatisfied with Wix in the way that someone who has been burned is dissatisfied. He simply did a financial review, looked at what R3,000 per month was producing, and reached a conclusion.

---

## The conversation that started the migration

Sipho is methodical. When we spoke, he asked me specific questions about the migration. What would happen to his existing content? How long would there be any downtime? What would the WordPress site do technically that his Wix site was not doing?

I walked him through the full comparison.

PageSpeed score: his current site at 39, the WordPress build typically launching at 88 to 95 on mobile.

Schema: his Wix site had no structured data. The WordPress build would have LocalBusiness schema identifying him as a ProfessionalService entity in Johannesburg, with his service areas, operating structure, and contact information.

Lead delivery: his Wix form routed to a business Gmail account that he checked twice a day. The WordPress form would route to WhatsApp and deliver submissions within 60 seconds of them being submitted.

Data: his Wix analytics were Wix's own proprietary analytics, which he found difficult to read and which did not integrate with Google Search Console. The WordPress site would have Google Site Kit connected to Search Console and Google Analytics, showing him exactly what Google knew about his site and how it was performing.

Ownership: his Wix content could not be exported in a usable format. His WordPress site would be backed up daily to his own Google Drive and could be moved to any hosting provider or handed to any developer, at any time, without restriction.

He listened to all of this and said: "And the total cost is R1,999 plus R299 per month?"

I confirmed.

He said: "I spent R90,000 on Wix over 30 months to have a site with a PageSpeed score of 39. This is straightforward."

---

## The build

Sipho's site structure was different from the trade-service builds I have described in other case studies. Coaching is a professional services category where the website needs to convey expertise, methodology, and credibility rather than urgent local need.

The build had five pages:

A homepage that established his positioning clearly: who he works with, what outcomes his clients achieve, and a testimonial from a named corporate client.

A services page with separate sections for executive coaching, team coaching, and leadership development workshops, each with a description that matched the language corporate buyers use when looking for this type of service.

A results page with three detailed case studies from past engagements. These were the most important pieces of content on the site. They described specific business contexts, specific leadership challenges, and specific measurable outcomes. They did not use generic phrases like "improved performance." They said things like "the leadership team reduced decision-making latency from 14 days to 3 days over six months" and "voluntary staff attrition in the team dropped from 34 percent to 11 percent in the 12 months following the engagement."

An about page with Sipho's full professional background, methodology, and credentials.

A contact page with a form routing to WhatsApp and a clear description of what happens after enquiry (an initial 30-minute discovery call, at no cost, to discuss the engagement).

The technical foundation was standard: PageSpeed 92 on mobile at launch, ProfessionalService schema on the homepage and services pages, Google Site Kit connected.

---

## After: R3,000 per month becomes R299 per month

The financial impact of the migration was immediate. Sipho's monthly platform spend went from R3,000 to R299. Over 24 months, that is a saving of R64,824.

The website cost R1,999 once-off. The payback period on that investment, purely from the monthly cost saving, was 19 days.

That calculation excludes any revenue generated by the site, which exists and is measurable. In the three months following launch, Sipho received 11 enquiries through the website. Six became discovery calls. Three became paid engagements.

His average engagement value is approximately R45,000. Three engagements in the first three months represents R135,000 in revenue that can be traced to the website.

He was not receiving any enquiries through his Wix site. The form had routed through Wix's mail relay, and when I checked the delivery logs during migration, it was clear that a significant proportion of submissions had not been delivered. He may have had more enquiries that never reached him, but the confirmed number before the migration was zero in the six months prior.

## The specific calculation for the reader

I am going to do the maths explicitly, because I think it is instructive.

Sipho's Wix Business plan: R3,000 per month.
Sipho's WordPress site: R1,999 once-off plus R299 per month.

The crossover point where the WordPress option becomes cheaper, counting from day one:

R1,999 divided by (R3,000 minus R299) equals R1,999 divided by R2,701, which equals 0.74 months, or approximately 22 days.

After 22 days, the WordPress option is already cheaper. For every month beyond that, Sipho saves R2,701.

Over five years, the total Wix cost at R3,000 per month is R180,000 with no asset at the end. The total WordPress cost is R1,999 plus 60 months at R299, which equals R19,939. With a fully owned, fully portable WordPress site at the end.

The difference is R160,061.
