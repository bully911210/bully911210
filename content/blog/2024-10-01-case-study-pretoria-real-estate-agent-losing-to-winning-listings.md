André Fouché has been a real estate agent in Pretoria East for seven years. He is, by any reasonable measure, good at his job. He knows his market. He has repeat clients who have used him for two and three transactions. His conversion rate on mandates he was given was strong.

The problem was getting the mandates.

When a homeowner in Pretoria East is considering listing their property, they do not open their contacts list and call agents they have never met. They search. They visit agency websites and individual agent profiles. They look at who appears to know the area, who has listed and sold similar properties, and who looks like the kind of professional they would trust with a transaction that, for most South Africans, is the largest financial decision they will ever make.

André was losing that first impression to competitors who had agency websites and personal profile pages that positioned them as established, knowledgeable, and present in the area. When a potential seller searched for a real estate agent in their Pretoria East suburb, André was not appearing. The agents who were appearing were not necessarily better than André. They were just findable.

---

## Before: doing good work that no one could discover

André had a profile on his agency's company website. It was accurate. It listed his credentials, his years of experience, and his contact number. It was one profile among forty on the same domain, on a corporate site that had not been updated in 18 months and that scored 44 on Google PageSpeed Mobile.

He had no personal website. He had a Facebook business page that he updated irregularly. He had a strong reputation among people who already knew him, and almost no presence for people who did not.

He had spent the previous two years building a Google Business profile, which had helped somewhat. But without a website linked to the profile, the profile could only confirm his existence. It could not demonstrate his expertise.

The gap that mattered: when a homeowner in Faerie Glen or Garsfontein searched for a real estate agent, the results were dominated by the large franchise agencies and their individual agents who had personal websites. André appeared, when he appeared at all, in a position too low to receive clicks.

---

## The build: structure for a high-credibility category

Real estate is a category where the website does a specific job. It does not need to close the transaction. It needs to demonstrate enough expertise and local knowledge to justify a phone call.

The site structure we built:

A homepage that established André as a Pretoria East specialist, with a current listings section (updated via a simple WordPress custom post type that André could update himself), a sold properties section with testimonials from sellers, and a clear call to action for mandate enquiries.

Three suburb-specific pages: Faerie Glen, Garsfontein, and Moreleta Park. Each page had 500 to 700 words of genuinely useful content about selling property in that suburb, including average days on market, typical buyer profile, and what factors affected sale price in that area. Each page had a URL structured as /real-estate-agent-[suburb] and each had schema identifying André as a RealEstateAgent operating in that suburb.

An about page with a full professional narrative written in André's voice, including a specific section on his seven years of market knowledge in the area.

The technical foundation was the same as every other build: PageSpeed 90 on mobile at launch, LocalBusiness schema on the homepage and suburb pages, WhatsApp form routing for mandate enquiries.

---

## After: winning mandates from people who had never heard of him

In the three months following launch, André received 14 mandate enquiries through the website. Of those 14, he won 9 mandates.

The average mandate value for a property transaction in Pretoria East in this period was approximately R2.1 million in property price. At a commission rate of 5 percent plus VAT, each completed transaction represented approximately R120,750 in commission to the selling agent.

Of the 9 mandates, 5 went to successful sale within the first six months. That represents approximately R603,750 in commissions from leads directly attributed to the website in the first six months.

The website cost R1,999 once-off and R299 per month. The first successful transaction from a website lead covered the entire annual running cost of the site in one commission.

---

## The bridge: what changed between losing and winning

Before the website, André was an agent who existed in his market. After the website, he was an agent who was present, specific, and demonstrably knowledgeable about three specific suburbs in his market.

The suburb pages were the single most important element of the build. When a homeowner in Garsfontein searched for "real estate agent Garsfontein," André's Garsfontein page appeared because it was the most specific piece of content on the internet about being a real estate agent in Garsfontein. No franchise website had a page dedicated to that suburb. André did.

Specificity wins local searches. A single-suburb page with 600 words of genuine local knowledge outranks a generic agency page with a suburb name in the header.

The second important element was the social proof section. André had strong testimonials from past clients. On the agency website, these were buried. On his personal site, they appeared on the homepage and on each suburb page. They made the difference between a potential client who clicked away and one who submitted a mandate enquiry.

---

## What this looks like for any high-consideration service

The real estate case study applies to any service where the client is making a high-stakes decision and doing research before they make contact. Accountants. Financial advisers. Family law attorneys. Architects. Interior designers. Wedding planners.

In each of these categories, the website is not selling a transaction. It is providing enough evidence of expertise and local relevance to justify a conversation. The three elements that do this most effectively are: suburb-specific pages that answer specific local questions, testimonials that describe specific outcomes, and content that demonstrates genuine knowledge of the market.

These are not difficult to build. They are simply not present in most small business websites.

That shift, from fighting for referrals among people who already know you to being found by people who specifically searched for what you offer, is what a correctly built website produces.
